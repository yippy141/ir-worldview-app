# Copy Tone Guide — Phase 6C.1

## What we are aiming for

The product should sound like:
- a sharp human editor
- calm, clear, and confident
- accessible without being simplistic
- serious without sounding like a graduate seminar note

The product should **not** sound like:
- a model nervously explaining itself
- a taxonomy engine narrating category logic
- a policy memo compressed into one sentence
- a generic AI explainer repeating “X matters” and “you think…”

## Good sentence patterns

Prefer patterns like:
- “You usually start with…”
- “You are more likely to…”
- “You are less likely to assume…”
- “This pattern usually treats…”
- “When questions become more concrete, this pattern often…”
- “Under pressure, this pattern often shifts toward…”
- “A user in this pattern often worries that…”
- “This profile is often pulled between…”

## Avoid these patterns

Avoid or strongly reduce:
- “X matters”
- “you think X matters”
- “you place real emphasis on…”
- “nearest-fit shorthand” on visible user-facing cards
- “broad-spectrum” on visible card surfaces unless clearly unpacked
- “issue read, not a mini-identity” above the fold
- “what it measured / what it did not claim” above the fold in Standard mode
- repeated “conditional,” “selective,” and “mixed” without concrete unpacking

## Card-writing rules

### For Atlas cards

A card should answer these questions fast:
1. What kind of pattern is this?
2. What usually drives it?
3. How does it often shift under pressure?

A card summary should:
- be one sentence
- use plain English
- avoid stacked clauses
- avoid dense nominal phrases
- avoid sounding like a stitched-together memo

### For Atlas detail pages

The top block should answer:
- what this pattern looks like in practice
- what often pulls it in this direction
- where it is often confused with neighbors

## Rewrite examples

### Too coded
“This pattern treats leverage, dependence, and hierarchy as active drivers of state behavior.”

### Better
“This pattern often starts with leverage and dependence rather than assuming the system is neutral.”

### Too coded
“Institutions matter when they can carry coordination.”

### Better
“This pattern trusts institutions most when they can actually hold coordination together.”

### Too coded
“You think legitimacy matters, but only under some conditions.”

### Better
“You usually keep legitimacy in view, but not at the cost of ignoring power or risk.”

### Too coded
“Competition, conditionally.”

### Better
“You take rivalry seriously, but you are not automatically looking for the hardest line.”

## Visual-summary rules

When a visual already shows the pattern shape, the text should not re-explain every dimension.
Use the text to interpret the shape, not duplicate it.

Bad pattern:
- chart says X
- bullets say X again
- paragraph says X a third time

Better pattern:
- chart shows X
- bullets highlight the two strongest signals
- paragraph explains what that combination usually means
