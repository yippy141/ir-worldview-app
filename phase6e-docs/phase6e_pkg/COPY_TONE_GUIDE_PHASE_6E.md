# Copy Tone Guide — Phase 6E

## Voice target

The product should sound like:
- a sharp public-facing editor
- a policy explainer written for an intelligent generalist
- confident enough to be useful, but not inflated

It should not sound like:
- an internal PM/spec memo
- a model narrating its own uncertainty
- a grad seminar prompt that escaped into the UI
- a personality-test app with academic jargon sprinkled on top

## Tone rules

### 1. User-first, not system-first

Prefer:
- You usually start with strategic pressure before you move to norms or reassurance.
- In technology, you become more comfortable with targeted controls than your baseline alone would suggest.

Avoid:
- The integrated payoff is not a cleaner box.
- The model is synthesizing cross-domain overlays.
- The nearest-fit shorthand remains active.

### 2. Plain English before taxonomy

Prefer:
- You trust coordination more than doctrinal confrontation.
- You do not assume reassurance works on its own.

Avoid:
- Institutionalism remains live as a runner-up orientation.
- This reflects a bounded coalition-minded tilt.

### 3. Say what changes, not just that it changes

Prefer:
- In security, you accept harder deterrence than your baseline would suggest.
- In technology, you keep access and safety in view even when competition sharpens.

Avoid:
- The overlay meaningfully diverges.
- The issue file reveals a domain-conditioned shift.

### 4. Avoid AI-coded phrasing

Avoid or reduce these patterns:
- X matters
- you think X matters
- X remains live
- X stays in the frame
- nearest-fit shorthand
- controlled synthesis
- integrated payoff
- broad-spectrum baseline
- true tension
- case by case
- mixed normative style

### 5. Use short sentences above the fold

Top blocks should be readable in one pass.
If a sentence runs past ~22 words, check whether it can be split.

### 6. Keep Standard and Deep-dive separate

Standard mode:
- plainer
- shorter
- fewer labels
- fewer caveats

Deep-dive:
- more nuance
- more methodology
- more taxonomy / evidence detail

## Preferred sentence patterns

Good patterns:
- You usually start with...
- You are more likely to trust...
- You are less willing to assume...
- This stays stable even when...
- This is where your answers split...
- When the issue becomes concrete, you shift toward...
- The main thing to pressure-test is...

## Example rewrites

Bad:
- A controlled synthesis of the Foundation baseline and the completed focus-area overlays on this device.
Good:
- How your baseline changes once the questions become more concrete.

Bad:
- The integrated payoff is not a cleaner box.
Good:
- Your answers do not collapse into one neat doctrine once the domain changes.

Bad:
- Technology becomes more coordination-minded when the question turns from explanation to policy choice.
Good:
- In technology, you explain competition one way but choose more coordinated rules when you have to pick a policy.

Bad:
- Security read: bounded pressure, case by case.
Good:
- In security, you prefer targeted pressure over either passivity or maximal escalation.

Bad:
- This lane keeps coordination, access, and safety in the frame at the same time.
Good:
- You do not reduce AI governance to national control alone; access and safety still stay in view.

## Editing priority

If time is limited, prioritize:
1. Profile top block
2. Foundation result top block
3. Module result top block
4. Public home hero / Explore / Methods
5. Atlas detail summaries
