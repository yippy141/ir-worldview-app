AI Atlas browse — two directions

Two compact takes on the AI Atlas browse map. Six archetypes, named definition, one nearby neighbor, 1–2 differentiators each, and a single obvious route to the AI Field Guide. Same paper, ink and accents as the rest of the product.

01 Index Shelf Three-column gridded shelf · uniform cells · library register
Why this works A 3×2 grid of equal-weight cards, ruled like a printed index. Each card carries an archetype number, axis tag, name, one-line definition, two differentiators, and a nearby neighbor — nothing else. No fingerprint chart on the card; that detail lives one click away in the AI Field Guide. The flatness is the point: every archetype gets the same air, so scanning is a horizontal sweep across uniform cells rather than a comparison between unequal posters.

Recommendation for fast scanning

Which direction to ship

01 · Index Shelf

Wins on calm. Six identical cells, no ornament, every archetype gets the same air. Best for a reader who arrives without a question and wants to read the patterns sequentially.

Loses on at-a-glance comparison. The reader has to read text to tell two archetypes apart; nothing distinguishes them visually except their words.

02 · Fingerprint Row

Wins on scanning. The four-row fingerprint turns each archetype into a recognizable shape — Strategic Competitor reads as “tall capability, short openness” before the eye reaches the prose. Two passes get you a working map.

Loses on density. The wider 2-up grid means the reader sees three archetypes per fold rather than six; comparison is faster but coverage is slower.

For a browse page — where the user is sweeping to find the one or two archetypes that match a real question — Direction 02 (Fingerprint Row) is the better choice. The fingerprint encodes the differentiators visually, so the eye does the comparison and the text confirms; Direction 01 makes the eye work through prose. Direction 01 is the better fallback if the team decides differentiator language, not shape, is the editorial priority — but for fast scanning of six items, shape wins.
