# IR APP EDITORIAL STYLE GUIDE

## Tone target
Think:
- The Economist
- Eurasia Group briefing note
- DGA-style client memo

Do not think:
- lecture notes
- dissertation abstract
- startup landing page
- therapy app

## Core rules

### 1. Write for fast comprehension
A user should understand the sentence on first read.
If they need to read it twice, simplify it.

### 2. Use one idea per sentence
Bad:
- "International institutions can meaningfully constrain powerful states by altering expectations, reducing uncertainty, and creating repeated incentives for compliance over time."

Better:
- "Institutions matter most when they reduce uncertainty and make non-compliance costly."

### 3. Prefer concrete nouns and verbs
Prefer:
- power
- trade
- leverage
- trust
- threat
- rules
- pressure
- bargaining

Avoid stacking abstractions like:
- legitimacy architecture
- normative configuration
- systemic conditionality
- strategic disposition in practice

### 4. Avoid filler phrases
Cut unless necessary:
- fundamentally
- increasingly
- in many ways
- in practice
- broadly speaking
- complex
- nuanced
- dynamic
- multifaceted
- significant

### 5. Hide the theory, don’t hide the meaning
The question text should be readable without prior theory training.
The disclosure can explain:
- what school of thought the item taps
- what key term means
- what the question is not asking

### 6. Avoid fake precision
Do not write:
- "This reveals your deep underlying worldview architecture"
- "This precisely captures your strategic orientation"

Write:
- "This suggests which line of argument you tend to find more convincing"
- "This result is a structured interpretation, not a verdict"

### 7. Don’t sound like AI
Avoid:
- self-discovery language
- universal platitudes
- overbalanced “both/and” phrasing
- sentences that sound polished but empty

## Question-writing rules
- 12–24 words is ideal
- one claim, not two or three
- if a technical term is necessary, keep it to one term max
- if a sentence contains three abstract nouns, rewrite it

## Result-writing rules
A result section should answer one of these questions:
- What line of argument do I trust more?
- Where am I mixed?
- What changed my result?
- What should I not overread from this?

## Examples

Bad:
- "State interests are constituted through social interaction rather than existing in a fixed, exogenous form."

Better:
- "States do not walk into world politics with fixed interests; interaction helps shape what they want."

Bad:
- "Sovereignty should yield to wider moral obligations when sufficiently grave humanitarian protections are implicated."

Better:
- "In extreme cases, protecting civilians can matter more than strict non-intervention."
