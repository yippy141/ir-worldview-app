# AI Governance Compass Results V2 Brainstorm

## What feels thin in the current results

From the current result PDFs, the page already has a good high-level shell:

- strong archetype title
- three modifier pills
- short explanation paragraph
- axis score cards
- runner-up archetype

What is missing is **interpretive depth**.

Right now the user learns:

- what type they are
- where they scored on each axis
- who the runner-up is

But they do **not** yet get a satisfying answer to the deeper questions that make result pages memorable:

- What do I actually optimize for?
- What policies would someone like me probably support?
- What do people like me get wrong?
- What kind of coalition am I implicitly building?
- What would change my mind?
- Where do my values come into tension under pressure?
- How am I different from the next-closest archetype?
- What does this imply for U.S.-China, middle powers, and global governance?

## Main design recommendation

Do not try to make the result page longer by simply adding more axis blurbs.

Instead, make it feel like a **policy profile** with a clear narrative arc:

1. Who you are
2. What you optimize for
3. What policies follow from that
4. Where your worldview strains under pressure
5. Who you are next to, and who you are farthest from
6. What evidence would shift your position

That is the right balance between rigorous and shareable.

## Recommended V2A result-page structure

### 1. Hero block

Keep:

- archetype title
- three pills
- one-paragraph summary

Add:

- clarity or hybrid signal
- one-line governing instinct

Examples:

- `Clear tilt: Coordination Architect`
- `Hybrid zone: Precautionary Steward / Democratic Guardrailist`
- `Governing instinct: slow diffusion before institutions lose control`

## 2. Governing instinct

A short, high-signal paragraph answering:

- What is the first thing this worldview protects?
- What failure mode is it most afraid of?
- What tradeoff is it most willing to accept?

This is the section most likely to make people say: `yes, that feels like me`.

## 3. Likely policy package

This should be a card grid, not one long paragraph.

Suggested cards:

- Frontier release thresholds
- Public oversight and enforcement
- International order and standards
- Open vs controlled access
- Military use and red lines
- Future society and human control

Each card should say:

- likely instinct
- why someone with this worldview goes there

This makes the page feel much more concrete.

## 4. International order implications

This is where the project can stand out.

Add a dedicated section that translates the archetype into positions on:

- U.S.-China competition vs coordination
- club governance vs broad legitimacy
- middle-power dependence and sovereignty
- whether developing countries get voice and access

This section is especially important given your own China and IR angle.

## 5. Pressure points and internal tensions

The current tensions section is a good start, but it should not appear only for some profiles.

Every result should have this section.

Good tension cards sound like this:

- You want caution, but you also fear incumbent capture.
- You want stronger state oversight, but you may not trust existing state capacity.
- You want coordination, but real coordination may still reproduce hierarchy.
- You want openness, but openness raises the burden on misuse controls.

This section makes the result feel smarter and more honest.

## 6. Why not the runner-up?

The current runner-up card is useful but underdeveloped.

Add one short comparison paragraph:

- what separates the primary type from the runner-up
- which axes made the difference
- whether the user is a clear case or a hybrid case

This makes the result feel more personalized.

## 7. Most distant archetype

This is one of the easiest ways to make the result more interesting.

Add a small `Most distant from` card.

Why it works:

- it sharpens the user's self-understanding
- it is highly shareable
- it makes the archetype space feel more real

Use a soft tone. Avoid `enemy` framing.

## 8. What would change your mind

This is one of the best additions.

It turns the result from a static label into a real worldview map.

Examples:

- stronger evidence that controlled deployment actually reduces systemic risk
- stronger evidence that overcentralization mainly entrenches incumbents
- stronger evidence that rivals will exploit unilateral restraint
- stronger evidence that public institutions cannot absorb the tempo of capability change

This section is especially good for expert audiences.

## 9. Shareable summary line

Add a quote-like line for social sharing.

Examples:

- `You optimize for institutional control before frontier capability escapes the guardrails.`
- `You think AI governance only becomes legitimate when power is shared beyond labs.`
- `You treat rivalry as real, but not as an excuse to abandon coordination.`

This can power a future share card.

## What to postpone to V2B

Do not overload V2A with everything you eventually want.

These should come later because they need new questions or richer payload data:

- transhumanism and augmentation stance
- redistribution and political economy
- centralization vs decentralization overlay
- sentient-life and moral-circle questions
- exact scenario-choice recap
- compare-with-friends and matrix views
- Chinese-language or bilingual mode

## How to treat the philosophical material

Topics like:

- The Egg
- sentient life
- AI-human merging
- human purpose
- techno-optimism vs skepticism

are all real parts of the broader discourse.

But for product design, they should be handled as **overlay worldview badges**, not as the main archetype engine yet.

Why:

- they cut across the main governance disputes
- they make the main archetypes muddy if introduced too early
- they deserve their own careful question wording

Good overlay clusters for later:

- Augmentation stance
- Governance architecture
- Distribution and ownership
- Moral circle and personhood

## What will make this product stand out

Not another personality toy.

The distinctive version is:

- serious enough for policy people
- legible enough for general users
- explicitly international
- comfortable with U.S.-China and Global South questions
- willing to name tensions, not just flatter the user

The result page should feel like:

- a polished profile
- a worldview mirror
- a conversation starter
- a credible portfolio piece

not just a score report.
